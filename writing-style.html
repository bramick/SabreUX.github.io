Abbreviations and acronyms

Abbreviations

An abbreviation is a shortened or contracted form of a word or phrase that represents the whole, as in Mon. for Monday, Dr. for doctor, and NCAA for National Collegiate Athletic Association. You do not pronounce an abbreviation by its shortened word form.

API application programming interface

mph miles per hour

MB/s megabytes per second

Use periods when abbreviating U.S. for United States within text (n headlines, do not use periods).

Use all caps, but no periods, in longer abbreviations when individual letters are pronounced, such as GDS. Unless a common phrase or proper name, such as FBI, spell out phrase and put abbreviation in parentheses for first reference.

Note — Do not refer to our products or services with abbreviations, e.g., Sabe Dev Studio as "SDS." Use the full name; or after the first use, "Dev Studio". Read more about styling our company names and terms.

Acronyms

An acronym is a word formed from the initial letters in a set of words; acronyms often are all caps, and you pronounce the word form as a separate word. In our communications, never substitute an acronym for Sabre names or terms.

ROM read-only memory

WAC Women's Army Corps

OPEC Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries

Capitalization

Capitalize only the first word in sentences and proper nouns.

Company and overall Sabre Red names.

Product and capability names.

Sabre Red brand treatments.

Headings and headlines

Capitalize only the first word and pronouns.

For SEO reasons, ensure you've used keywords and theme words in headings h1, h2, and h3, and in the first few words of your first paragraph.

Lists

Lists are best introduced by a complete grammatical sentence, followed by a colon (except in the cases shown below). If introducing a list with a fragment, omit the colon (:).

All list items should be alike—all should be noun forms, phrases, full sentences, or whatever the context.

List items carry no closing punctuation unless they consist of complete sentences. Start list items with a capital letter.

Your sandwich must include these parts:

  • Wheat bread
  • Green tomato

Our platform is a place where

  • Coders can develop apps for travel agents.
  • Agents exchange feedback and ideas for applications.

Numbered lists

A period (.) follows the numeral and each item starts with a capital letter.

Follow these steps to order your sandwich online:

  1. Log in to the food page
  2. Click Order now
  3. Select Sandwiches to browse available options

Instructional or reference lists

Descriptive words identifying the subject matter may introduce list items. These descriptive words should match the context or use bold typeface, followed by a period.

  • code text. The content in this list item relates to code.
  • Introductory words. Format descriptive words in bold with an ending period .

Names and terms

Company and overall Sabre Red names. First use of these names (other than headlines, subheads, titles and images), such as Sabre®, Sabre Travel Network® and the Sabre® Red™ travel solution, should use capitalization, italics and registration symbols. Subsequent use of names may omit symbols but should still retain the “Sabre” descriptor and full name, capitalization and italics. These names should never hold a possessive mark.

Product and capability names. First use (other than headlines, subheads and titles) should have names appear exactly as listed in our naming guide, including capitalization, italics and registration symbols. Subsequent use may omit symbols and the “Sabre” descriptor. It should still retain the rest of its full name, capitalization and italics. For example, first use is Sabre® Traveler Security. Subsequent use is Traveler Security.

Sabre Red brand treatments . First use (other than headlines, subheads and titles) should be Sabre® Red™ travel solution, Sabre® Red™ Workspace, Sabre® Red™ App Centre, and Sabre® Red™ Apps. Subsequent use may omit the “Sabre” descriptor and marks, but still retain the “Red” descriptor. For example, first use is Sabre® Red™ App Centre. Subsequent use is Red App Centre.

Sabre Dev Studio is never abbreviated. After first use, you may drop the Sabre and use Dev Studio for the remainder of the webpage content.

Do not use italics or symbols in headlines, subheads, titles, or images.

First use of names. Treat each webpage like a new chapter — follow the guidelines for first-use per page.

Web. The Web is the Internet's most used service.

  • If used as an adjective and standing alone, capitalize Web. Also capitalize Internet.
  • Do not capitalize Web if used in a compound word.

Web, Web feed, and Web page

webcam, webcast, webmaster, webinar, and website

Web page URLs. Used in instructional or reference content, display the full URL www.urlinfo.com/ if necessary for context. Drop the www in informative content.

E-word. Hyphenate words like e-business, e-catalog, and e-commerce. Do not hyphenate conventional terms like email.

Numbers

Spell out numbers when

  • using zero through nine, unless the number is part of a measurement or approved standard (e.g., four bottles, 3 lb.)
  • starting a sentence with a number (e.g., "Twenty years ago, we moved to Texas").
  • expressing the zeros in million, billion, etc., following a numeral (e.g., 1 billion APIs...)

Use numerals when

  • displaying numbers 10 through 999,999
  • expressing a part of a measurement (e.g., 88 mph)
  • referencing an approved standard (e.g., layer 6 of ISO reference...)
  • expressing age; use hyphens when number is an adjective (e.g., Sue is 5 years old; She's a 5-year-old kid).

Percentages. For web content, use numeral with the percent symbol (e.g., 3%).

Commas. After the number 1,000, place commas between groups of three digits, starting from the right (e.g., 2,445,332).

Dates and times

Years are expressed in numerals unless they begin a sentence.

Format. Avoid all-numeric dates (incorrect date: 10/05/2000). Spell out or abbreviate the month.

  • do not use ordinals in dates — 15th April or April 15th is incorrect.
  • 15 April 2000 for instructional, reference, or back-end content that reaches international audiences.
  • April 15, 2000 for all content types, and content intended for American audiences. In text, add a comma or period after the year.

Use a hyphen in date adjectives (e.g., a three- to five-year plan...).

Use an en dash (–) between ranges of numbers (e.g., April 15–17). Read about the en dash.

Time. Use numerals except for noon and midnight (e.g., 8 a.m., noon, 14:00, 5:30 p.m.).

  • 12-hour or 24-hour format. Use the format best suited for context. If using 12-hour format, follow the hour numeral with a space then a.m. or p.m.
  • Time + units. After the numeral, spell out units of time (e.g., 50 hours, 23 minutes)

Time zones. Use zone name without reference to daylight savings or standing time (e.g., Pacific, Mountain, Central, Eastern, etc.)

Punctuation

Apostrophe (')

Plurals and possessives. Form the possessive singular of nouns with 's. Follow this rule whatever the final consonant.

Charles's friend
Burns's poems
the witch's malice

Em dash (—)

Usage. The most versatile of dashes. Use an em dash to amplify or explain, to separate a subject (or series of subjects) from a pronoun, to indicate a sudden break, and much more.

  • Add spaces before or after the em dash — this rule complies with the AP Stylebook).
  • Sentences can contain up to two em dashes.

En dash (–)

Usage. Used between two numbers, and means up to and including or through.

  • place an en dash between ranges of numbers, with spaces on both sides of it (e.g., 2000 – 2012).
  • omit the en dash if you write from, to, between within the number range (e.g., from 45 to 90). This option is less precise.

Hyphen (-)

Use hyphens in less common modified terms, like e-business, e-catalog and e-commerce.

Content and page elements

Headline. Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. No periods. Start with present-tense verb. Do not use italics or symbols in headlines, subheads, titles or images.

Subhead. Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. Can be a statement or question. Pull users in, and connect point to the headline. Do not use italics or symbols.

Body text. Use we and our Headings within body text vary—see templates below.

First use of names. Treat each webpage like a new chapter—follow the guidelines for first-use per page.

Search engine optimization

Include one key word or phrase per page, and mentioned in most or all of these elements:

  • Page title
  • Major <h1>, <h2>, or <h3> headings
  • The words that appear early in titles, and near the beginnings of the top five paragraphs of text
  • The alternate (“alt”) text of all content images
  • The tags (if any) in the header section of the page html code
  • The words in the html file name, separated with hyphens: “files-names-also-matter.html”

Page titles

The page title becomes the page's primary descriptor, so page titles should

  • contain carefully chosen keywords and themes for the page
  • form a concise, plainly worded description of the page contents
  • be unique, if possible

Page title also becomes the bookmark text, and some browsers truncate long page titles (more than 65 characters with spaces).
